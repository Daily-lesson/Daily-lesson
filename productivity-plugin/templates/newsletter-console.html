▏Executive Summary

The U.S. fab build-out has crossed from concrete to cadence. Texas Instruments has moved its $60B Sherman "mega-fab" (SM1) into volume production, and the story this quarter is no longer capacity — it is autonomy density: how many wafer moves, inspections, and tool-to-tool handoffs happen with zero human contact. GlobalFoundries and Siemens have formalized an AI-driven manufacturing alliance pushing intelligence to the fab floor, while the wafer-handling layer standardizes on direct-drive SCARA and vacuum end-effectors rated to ISO Class 1. Below: the developments that matter, and the numbers underneath them.

§01

Texas Instruments — Sherman Comes Online

300mm capacity · AMHS backbone · sensor-level robotics

Fab Capex CH·01
SM1 / TX

SM1 reaches volume production three-and-a-half years after groundbreaking

TI has begun volume production at its newest 300mm facility in Sherman, TX — the first of four planned fabs on the site and the anchor of a $60B, seven-fab program across Texas and Utah. At full ramp the campus is engineered to output tens of millions of analog and embedded-processing chips per day; analysts project Sherman alone could hold roughly a fifth of global 300mm analog capacity by 2030. Running a fab of this cadence is a materials-logistics problem first: every wafer lot moves under automation.

AMHS CH·02
OHT / RAIL

The overhead rail is the real production line

Behind the tool sets, a ceiling-mounted Automated Material Handling System does the work of the fab floor: standardized FOUPs carrying 25 wafers ride an overhead hoist-transport (OHT) rail network between process tools, eliminating cleanroom floor traffic and human contact with in-process wafers. For TI, AMHS throughput and routing intelligence — not just tool count — set the effective ceiling on how fast SM1 can ramp against demand.

Sensor · Silicon CH·03
EDGE / MCU

TI's other robotics play: the chips inside the robots

TI's automation footprint isn't only in its own fabs — its analog and embedded portfolio is a building block for the robots doing the handling. Real-time C2000 motor-control MCUs, mmWave radar for presence and collision sensing, and precision signal-chain parts are exactly the sensor-and-actuation layer that direct-drive wafer robots and mobile cobots depend on. The vertical loop is tightening: chips that move wafers, made in fabs that move wafers autonomously.

§02

GlobalFoundries — Intelligence Moves to the Floor

The Siemens alliance · "Physical AI" at the edge

AI · Edge CH·04
GF × SIE

Siemens + GlobalFoundries formalize an AI-manufacturing alliance

Announced in December 2025, the collaboration pairs GF's manufacturing scale with Siemens' automation, electrification, and digital-twin software stack — spanning chip design through product-lifecycle management. The thesis is "Physical AI": pushing compute intelligence off the central MES and onto edge robotics and tools on the floor, so decisions about routing, inspection, and fault response happen where the wafer is. The partners frame it as resilience for the global supply chain, with spillover into robotics and energy systems.

Fab Logistics CH·05
AMHS / MKT

Foundries are now the dominant buyer of 300mm AMHS

OHT held the largest equipment-type revenue share of the semiconductor AMHS market in 2025, and foundries — GF among the named expansion leaders alongside TSMC and Samsung — retained the dominant end-user position. Translation: the capacity race is being fought as much on rail-network density and carrier-routing software as on lithography. GF's floor-automation and edge-AI investments are the logistics half of that equation.

§03

Cleanroom Automation — The Sub-Micron Frontier

Direct-drive SCARA · vacuum handling · the ISO Class 1 mandate

Robotics Trend CH·06
SCARA / DD

Direct-drive SCARA becomes the default wafer mover

The industry is standardizing on direct-drive SCARA arms for wafer handling: no geared transmissions means fewer wear particles, higher stiffness, single-digit-micron repeatability, and faster accel/decel — all of which convert directly into yield and throughput. Paired with vacuum and edge-grip end-effectors and mobile cleanroom cobots, these systems shuttle 300mm (and emerging 450mm) wafers from FOUP to chamber while meeting ISO Class 1–5 contamination limits.

Contamination CH·07
ISO CLASS 1

ISO Class 1 is the engineering constraint that drives everything

At the strict end — ASML's EUV cleanrooms hold ISO Class 1, permitting roughly 10 particles of 0.1 micron per cubic meter — every robot is a potential contamination source. That single constraint is why the floor is going lubricant-free and human-free: direct-drive motion, sealed bearings, and mobile cobots (e.g. KUKA's KMR iiwa class) replace both gear trains and operators. Contamination budget, not reach or payload, is now the primary spec.
